More than 75% (354/472) of New York City subway stations are inaccessible.

I've lived in N.Y.C. for the past three years, and I haven't taken the subway once. Everyone complains about how much they hate it—I would probably hate it too—but I should have the option to decide that for myself.

I can't get off a subway and assume that it has an elevator, I don't want to get stuck somewhere.

As I've lived here, I've noticed that restaurants, stores, apartment buildings, name a type of place people go to, are completely inaccessible. I can go on Yelp and see if it's accessible, but based on my experiences, an able-bodied person tag places as accessible. Sure, it's great to have an elevator, but if I have to get up stairs to get to the elevator, it's not accessible.

I haven't found a list of inaccessible places, so I'm starting one—follow @inaccessibleearth for that list.

The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 has been law since 1990—why haven't these places been updated in the past 30 years?!

I designed this sign to call out these places.

If you know an inaccessible place and want to put a sign up, email me, I'll send one to you. All I ask is that you send me an image of the sign at the place and its location.

If you don't want to put a sign up, but still want to help, email me a photo of the inaccessible place, tell me it's location, and I'll add it to @inaccessibleearth.
